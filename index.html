What IS Pi, Anyway?

Pi is the ratio of a circle's circumference (the distance around) to its diameter (the distance across). No matter how big or small the circle, this ratio is always the same!

The Monte Carlo Dart Circus

Here's a wild idea: you can estimate Pi by randomly throwing darts at a square with a circle inscribed in it! The ratio of darts inside the circle to total darts approximates Pi/4.

Why? Area of circle = Pi*r^2. Area of square = (2r)^2 = 4r^2. Ratio = Pi/4!

The Chudnovsky Algorithm: Pi's Final Boss

The Chudnovsky formula is the heavyweight champion of Pi computation. Almost every modern world record for computing Pi has used some variant of it. Here's the full beast:

1/Pi = 12 · Σk=0..inf (-1)k · (6k)! · (13591409 + 545140134k) ⁄ ( (3k)! · (k!)3 · 6403203k+3/2 )

Why Is It So Fast?

Each single term of this series gives you about 14.18 new correct digits of Pi. Compare that to Leibniz, where you need about 5 billion terms just to get 10 digits right. With Chudnovsky, 10 digits takes... one term.

The Origin Story

Brothers David and Gregory Chudnovsky developed this formula in 1988. They were mathematicians working out of Gregory's apartment in New York City, where they built a custom supercomputer from mail-order parts. Using their formula, they computed Pi to over 1 billion digits - a world record at the time.

Their formula is actually a refinement of work by the legendary Indian mathematician Srinivasa Ramanujan, who in 1914 discovered a family of rapidly converging series for Pi. Ramanujan's original series gave ~8 digits per term. The Chudnovsky brothers supercharged it.

Where Do Those Magic Numbers Come From?

The constants 640320, 13591409, and 545140134 look like someone smashed a keyboard, but they actually come from deep number theory:

  • 640320 is related to the j-invariant of an elliptic curve with complex multiplication. Specifically, 6403203 = 262537412640768000, and the fact that ePi*sqrt(163) is almost an integer (off by ~10-12) is deeply connected to this value.
  • 163 is a Heegner number - one of only 9 special numbers where imaginary quadratic fields have unique factorization. The largest Heegner number gives the fastest convergence, and 163 is the biggest one. Lucky us!
  • 13591409 and 545140134 are derived from modular forms and the arithmetic of the same elliptic curve. They determine exactly where the series "aims" at Pi.

How It's Actually Implemented

In practice, nobody evaluates the formula naively term-by-term (those factorials get enormous). Instead, a technique called binary splitting is used:

  1. Split the summation range [0, N) in half recursively
  2. At each level, combine partial products instead of computing full factorials
  3. Use FFT-based multiplication for the huge numbers (millions of digits)
  4. At the end, do one giant division to get the final result

This reduces the complexity from O(N2) to O(N log2N · log(log N)), which matters when N is in the trillions.

Fun Fact: Google used the Chudnovsky algorithm (with y-cruncher software) to compute Pi to 105 trillion digits in 2024. The computation took about 75 days and required ~1 petabyte of storage. The Chudnovsky brothers' apartment probably couldn't handle that.
